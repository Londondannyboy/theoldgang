# The Old Gang — Premise

## Logline
A young detective joins a police station in Elephant and Castle just as regeneration demolition works disturb an unconsecrated grave — releasing a Victorian street gang from the dead, whose escalating violence only she can see for what it is, while uncovering that her own father's death is entangled with a far older and more powerful criminal organisation buried beneath Southwark.

## Genre
Crime / Supernatural / Literary — Peaky Blinders meets Life on Mars meets Gangs of London

## Influences
- Life on Mars (time displacement, police procedural)
- Peaky Blinders (period gang culture, visual style)
- Gangs of London (modern multicultural gang dynamics)
- The Seventh Seal (Ingmar Bergman — the conversation with death)
- Charles Booth's Poverty Map (Victorian London documented street by street)
- The real criminal history of Elephant and Castle

## Central Question / Theme
The past is not dead. It's not even past. Regeneration — the demolition of old estates, the erasure of old communities, the glossy new builds — doesn't erase history. It disturbs it.

But the deeper theme is about WHO caused the suffering. The Victorian street gang — the Old Gang — are vicious, brutal, operating on a scale of violence modern people can barely imagine. But they have their own honour. They are Elephant's own. They are the PRODUCT of the conditions, not the cause.

The REAL Old Gang — the True Old Gang — are the ones who CREATED those conditions. The exploitation. The insane child mortality. Women having backstreet abortions. Most kids dying before age five. Poverty. Disease. The Thames was rancid. Life expectancy in the East End was around 26 years. And while all this happened, the rich got richer. The East India Company types, the City of London's institutional corruption — THEY are the real gang. They built the system that created the rookeries, the street gangs, the desperate poverty that Booth mapped in black.

And they're coming back. But unlike the street gang — who don't recognise cameras, who are obviously displaced in time — the True Old Gang are NOT stupid. They have different accents now. They adapt. They will walk amongst us. Because the City hasn't changed as much as it thinks it has. A Victorian exploitation merchant in a modern suit is indistinguishable from a modern exploitation merchant in a modern suit.

The story is about all the things every human knows but refuses to look at: crime exists, poverty exists, insane human exploitation exists. It existed then. It exists now. It never stopped. The Old Gang's return just makes the continuity visible.

## The Central Irony
Everyone interprets the Old Gang through modern frameworks. When gang members say "dead," police hear slang. When territory matches Booth's map, police see postcode wars. When a suspect doesn't acknowledge cameras, nobody notices. The dead are hiding in plain sight because the living can't imagine them.

## Tone & Mood
Gritty, atmospheric, grounded in the real Elephant and Castle — multicultural, rough, being scraped away and rebuilt. The supernatural should feel WRONG, not fantastical — persistent cold, absence that unsettles, violence from a different century. The old women are matter-of-fact, not mystical. The horror is quiet and creeping.

## Target Length
70,000–90,000 words. Conceived as at least two books — Book 1 (street gang) and Book 2 (True Old Gang / City layer).

## Sequel Potential (Book 2 / Series 2)
The True Old Gang fully operational. City-layer criminals including Ignatius Sancho loose in modern London. Sarah, estranged from police, allied with community and the Camberwell Man, confronts institutional corruption that has been running for centuries. The drill scene at Southwark Bridge is the hinge between books.
