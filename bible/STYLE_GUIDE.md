# Style Guide

## Point of View
**RESOLVED: Third person limited, rotating.**
- **Sarah** — primary POV (~70% of chapters). The reader knows what Sarah knows. Her isolation and growing perception are felt directly.
- **Victor (formerly Marcus / the Camberwell Man)** — secondary POV. His chapters show the Old Gang forming, his dual life, his revelation, the horse ride.
- **Young Diamond Anne** — secondary POV. Camden, the Goth Girl, the transformation.
- **Other** — occasional chapters from the Ginger Urchin (members' club), the Avenger (crossing the river), the White Youth (nightclub), or modern gang members. Used sparingly.

This rotating POV allows the Old Gang to be developed as characters the reader cares about independently of Sarah's investigation. The Peaky Blinders model: we see the gang on their own terms, not just through a detective's eyes.

## Tense
**Past tense.** The story is about the past infecting the present.

## Prose Style
- **Modern world:** Naturalistic, contemporary London. Gritty but not try-hard. Multicultural Elephant felt as lived-in.
- **Old Gang intrusions:** Prose goes COLD. Shorter sentences. Something slightly off in rhythm. Not Victorian language, but touched by something old.
- **The old women:** Warm, grounded, South London with decades of memory. Speak plainly about impossible things.
- **Sarah's interiority:** Increasingly fractured as old world infects her life.
- **The old police unit:** Slower, more tactile — weight of filing cabinet drawers, click of old phone, silence where notifications should be.
- **Late chapters:** Boundary between living and dead blurs in the writing itself. Quiet erosion.
- **Violence and blood:** When the Old Gang commit violence, describe it from INSIDE their sensory experience. The warmth of blood on cold skin. The smell of iron. The colour against dead flesh. Violence for the Old Gang is not just action — it is a sensory event, the most vivid connection to the living world they can experience. The reader should feel the dead person's experience of touching life through blood, and simultaneously feel the horror of what that means. Sympathy and fear. Both. Always.

## Things to Do
- Ground in real Elephant geography — real streets, estates, pubs
- Modern gangs fully realised — they understand what police don't
- Violence genuinely shocking — not horror-movie, but casual Victorian brutality
- Let supernatural build slowly — reader unsure for a long time
- Cold is the tell — consistent, subtle
- Research Victorian Southwark, Booth's map, the Forty Elephants, Necropolis Railway
- Track how each real-world anchor (harmonica lad, workhouses, School of Thieves) confirms Sarah's thesis

## Things to Avoid
- Don't make Old Gang cartoonish or zombie-like — they're people from a specific time
- Sarah is not a "chosen one" — she's a good detective who sees what others dismiss
- Don't explain the mechanism — "the demolition disturbed the grave" is enough
- Don't make modern gangs stereotypical
- Don't resolve ambiguity too early
- Don't reference Bergman, King Arthur, or Booth by name in the prose — all references should be felt, not stated

## Dialogue Rules
- Modern police: naturalistic, institutional, casually dismissive
- Modern gangs: authentic South London — not parody. Frightened people.
- Old women: South London, decades-deep. Warm. Unafraid.
- Old Gang: [TBD — Victorian slang? Eerily formal? Mostly silent?]
- Sarah: professional, precise, increasingly strained

## Chapter Structure
- 3,000–4,000 words typical
- Multiple scene breaks allowed
- End on unease, revelation, or escalation

## Screen-Ready Prose — Writing for Netflix

This novel is written as a novel FIRST — but structured and styled so that a Netflix commissioning executive reading it sees the series already. Every creative decision should serve the page AND the screen.

### Scene-Based Writing
- Write in SCENES, not in exposition. Each chapter should be a sequence of scenes with clear visual beginnings and endings.
- Describe action and environment the way a camera would see it — grounded, specific, from a defined perspective.
- Avoid extended internal monologue that can't be translated to screen. Sarah's interiority should be expressed through what she DOES, what she NOTICES, what she SAYS — not through paragraphs of thought. Keep interiority brief and sharp.
- When writing from the Old Gang's POV, make their sensory experience VISUAL — show them touching the warm wall in sunlight, show them stopping dead at a busker, show them staring at a pineapple in Tesco. The reader/viewer should SEE the wonder.

### Episode-Ready Chapter Endings
- Every chapter ends on a beat that makes you need the next one: a cliffhanger, a revelation, a visual image that burns.
- Chapter 1 ends on the case file. Chapter 2 ends on the cold. Chapter 9 ends on silence. Chapter 18 ends on blood and flowers simultaneously. Chapter 28 ends on fingers with rings moving in the earth. These are episode endings. Every chapter must hit this standard.

### Dialogue-Forward
- Scenes should be driven by dialogue wherever possible. The novel's best moments are dialogue: "That's the Old Gang." "Have you heard of us?" "Call me Victor." "You don't know what life is until you know death." "Even within the dead world, you can never be too dead." "Like my father?" "You reap what you sow."
- Every major character should have at least one line the audience will remember. These lines should feel natural in prose AND be quotable on screen.

### Visual Set-Pieces
- The novel contains sequences that are explicitly VISUAL — designed to be seen:
  - Victor on horseback across estate walkways
  - Diamond Anne's simultaneous transformation / old Diamond Anne's death
  - The Old Gang among costumed actors on the South Bank
  - John pulling the knife from Thames foreshore wood at low tide
  - The Ginger Urchin stepping out of the hollow tree
  - The drill scene at Southwark Bridge — BOOM — fingers with rings
  - The hospital reckoning — Urchin in a hoodie, patriarch begging
  - The Old Gang singing "Wot Cher!" at the Golden Eagle
  - Sarah looking at a Victorian photograph and recognising a face
- Write these with the PACE and CLARITY of a shot list. Short sentences. Precise images. No clutter.

### The Netflix Structure
- **Season 1 = Book 1** (the street gang, Sarah's father, 8-10 episodes)
- **Season 2 = Book 2** (the City, the guilds, the global reach, 8-10 episodes)
- Each episode = roughly 2-3 chapters from the outline
- The 28-chapter outline maps to approximately 10 episodes:
  - **Episode 1** (Ch 1-3): Sarah arrives, first body, the station, the Old Woman
  - **Episode 2** (Ch 4-5): Modern gangs, Victor's introduction
  - **Episode 3** (Ch 6-7): Kennish Man, Diamond Anne in Camden
  - **Episode 4** (Ch 8-9): Custody scene, blood test, banishment
  - **Episode 5** (Ch 10-12): Booth map, Old Gang reform, School of Thieves
  - **Episode 6** (Ch 13-14): Diamond Anne & Goth Girl, the phone
  - **Episode 7** (Ch 15-17): Nightclub, the files, Fagin returns
  - **Episode 8** (Ch 18-19): Diamond Anne transforms, Corrupt Policeman confrontation
  - **Episode 9** (Ch 20-24): Alliance, Victor's revelation, Urchin crosses river, Avenger goes in
  - **Episode 10** (Ch 25-28): Old crimes, father returns, the Conversation, THE DRILL

### Tone Reference for Screen
- **Peaky Blinders** — visual style, period gang culture, the slow-motion set-piece
- **Life on Mars** — time displacement, the detective out of their depth, institutional wrongness
- **Gangs of London** — modern multicultural gang dynamics, extreme violence that means something
- **Top Boy** — authentic South London, community, the smart gangster trying to get out
- **The Seventh Seal** — the conversation with death (thematic, never visual reference)
