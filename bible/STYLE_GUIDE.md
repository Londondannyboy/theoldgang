# Style Guide

## Point of View
TO BE DECIDED — candidates:
- **Third person limited, Sarah only** — reader knows only what Sarah knows. Revelations land with her. Isolation felt.
- **Third person limited, rotating** — Sarah primary, with chapters from modern gang members, old women, possibly Old Gang.
- **First person Sarah** — most intimate, heightens "is she losing it?" tension.

## Tense
**Past tense.** The story is about the past infecting the present.

## Prose Style
- **Modern world:** Naturalistic, contemporary London. Gritty but not try-hard. Multicultural Elephant felt as lived-in.
- **Old Gang intrusions:** Prose goes COLD. Shorter sentences. Something slightly off in rhythm. Not Victorian language, but touched by something old.
- **The old women:** Warm, grounded, South London with decades of memory. Speak plainly about impossible things.
- **Sarah's interiority:** Increasingly fractured as old world infects her life.
- **The old police unit:** Slower, more tactile — weight of filing cabinet drawers, click of old phone, silence where notifications should be.
- **Late chapters:** Boundary between living and dead blurs in the writing itself. Quiet erosion.

## Things to Do
- Ground in real Elephant geography — real streets, estates, pubs
- Modern gangs fully realised — they understand what police don't
- Violence genuinely shocking — not horror-movie, but casual Victorian brutality
- Let supernatural build slowly — reader unsure for a long time
- Cold is the tell — consistent, subtle
- Research Victorian Southwark, Booth's map, the Forty Elephants, Necropolis Railway
- Track how each real-world anchor (harmonica lad, workhouses, School of Thieves) confirms Sarah's thesis

## Things to Avoid
- Don't make Old Gang cartoonish or zombie-like — they're people from a specific time
- Sarah is not a "chosen one" — she's a good detective who sees what others dismiss
- Don't explain the mechanism — "the demolition disturbed the grave" is enough
- Don't make modern gangs stereotypical
- Don't resolve ambiguity too early
- Don't reference Bergman, King Arthur, or Booth by name in the prose — all references should be felt, not stated

## Dialogue Rules
- Modern police: naturalistic, institutional, casually dismissive
- Modern gangs: authentic South London — not parody. Frightened people.
- Old women: South London, decades-deep. Warm. Unafraid.
- Old Gang: [TBD — Victorian slang? Eerily formal? Mostly silent?]
- Sarah: professional, precise, increasingly strained

## Chapter Structure
- 3,000–4,000 words typical
- Multiple scene breaks allowed
- End on unease, revelation, or escalation
