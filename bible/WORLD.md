# World & Setting

## Primary Setting: Elephant and Castle, South London

Elephant and Castle is in the London Borough of Southwark, south of the Thames. It is undergoing one of London's most significant regeneration programmes — demolition of post-war council estates replaced with new-build developments.

**What matters for the story:**
- DEMOLITION is the inciting mechanism — disturbance of unconsecrated graves brings the Old Gang back
- The area is rough, multicultural, in genuine flux — old communities displaced by new money
- Deep criminal history going back centuries — Southwark was outside City jurisdiction, a haven for criminals, prostitutes, theatre, bear-baiting
- The older residents remember a different Elephant

**Key locations:**
- The police station — modern, functional. Contains the old unit Sarah's banished to.
- Demolished/demolishing estates — construction sites where graves are disturbed
- Old pubs — where Kennish Man picks fights
- Local community spaces — where the older women gather
- **The Elsbury Estate** — a real estate in Elephant and Castle, on the cusp of regeneration. The skinhead figure hangs about on its outskirts near a community gastropub. The estate represents the last of the old Elephant — about to be demolished, about to become new builds, about to erase another layer of the area's memory.
- **The regeneration construction sites** — where the Kennish Man works. Cranes, hoardings, hard hats. The dead labourer rebuilding the ground he was buried in.
- **Myatt's Field** — the area where Camberwell drifts into Brixton. Victor's home territory. A modern gang rivalry exists between Myatt's Field/Brixton and Elephant and Castle — a territorial boundary Victor crosses when he joins the Old Gang.
- **Loughborough Junction / Railway Arches** — where the community horse project operates, under the railway lines. Inner-city kids riding horses among the arches and estate blocks. Victor's starting point for the horse ride.
- **The Estate Walkways** — the covered landings, passages, and bridges between estate blocks at level one. Victor rides a horse across these — designed for foot traffic, now carrying a man on horseback heading to war.

## Real-World Anchors — Old Things Hiding in Plain Sight

### Charles Booth's Poverty Map (1886–1903)
**THE KEY PLOT DEVICE.** Booth and his researchers colour-coded every street in London by social class:
- **Yellow** — Upper-middle/upper class
- **Red** — Middle class
- **Pink/Purple** — Mixed
- **Light Blue** — Poor
- **Dark Blue** — Very poor
- **Black** — "Lowest class. Vicious, semi-criminal." — THE ROOKERIES

The Old Gang can ONLY operate within streets Booth coloured black/dark blue. Their territory IS the Victorian criminal geography. Modern police read it as postcode wars. Sarah overlays Booth's map on modern crime data — exact match — predicts next crime locations. She's right.

The Booth maps are held by the London School of Economics. The detail is street-level — THIS side but not THAT side. This granularity makes the device work.

### The Rookeries
Dense, overcrowded Victorian criminal slums — mazes of narrow alleys where the poorest lived. The black zones on Booth's map. The Old Gang's natural habitat.

### The London Necropolis Railway
A real railway (built 1854) specifically for transporting dead bodies from Waterloo to Brookwood Cemetery in Surrey. Ran for almost a century. Remnants still exist near Waterloo, still technically linked to the rail network. A dead railway for dead people.

**Function:** Someone traffics a body by rail from Waterloo — using infrastructure built for moving the dead.

### The Forty Elephants (Forty Thieves)
Real all-female crime gang based in Elephant and Castle (1870s–1950s). Led by figures like Alice Diamond. Organised shoplifting from West End stores. Sophisticated, disciplined, underestimated because they were women. The women outlived the men.

### The Harmonica Lad on Westminster Bridge (2024)
True event: a young lad from Leeds/Bradford playing harmonica on Westminster Bridge. Dickensian London in 2024. Sarah uses details like this to argue old things exist in plain sight.

### Workhouses and Modern Exploitation
Victorian workhouses concentrated in Southwark. Near Waterloo tracks, modern migrants live in identical conditions. The workhouse never closed — it stopped using the name.

### The School of Thieves
Victorian child-theft academies (the reality behind Dickens' Fagin) existed around Saffron Hill / Old Pie Corner. In the story, a MODERN School of Thieves operates in Elephant — children under 16 taught to steal in a modern building. Sarah finds it. Tells colleagues. They laugh. It's real.

### The Teddy Boys — Born in Elephant
Teddy Boy subculture originated in Elephant and Castle in the early 1950s. The name, the style, the gang violence — all invented here.

### The Hooligans — The Original Family
The word "hooligan" comes from the Hooligan family (Irish descent) in Elephant and Castle, 1890s.

### Ignatius Sancho and Black Georgian London
Ignatius Sancho (c.1729–1780) — first known Black British voter (1774), writer, composer, painted by Gainsborough. Black Londoners have been here for centuries.

### Elephant and Castle — A History of Criminal Firsts
- Forty Elephants (1870s–1950s)
- The Hooligans (1890s)
- The Teddy Boys (1950s)
- Significant Victorian street gang activity
- Proximity to the City of London

### Unconsecrated Burials
Used for criminals, suicides, prostitutes, the destitute. Southwark accumulated many such sites. Construction regularly uncovers remains. The grave near Southwark Bridge triggers the True Old Gang.

### Famous People from Elephant and Castle
- **Charlie Chaplin** — born in Walworth, 1889. Workhouse childhood.
- **Michael Faraday** — born in Newington Butts, 1791.
- **Charles Babbage** — connected to the area.
These figures can potentially return as part of the time-bleed.

### The Spiritual Connection — Sarah's Father and the Grave

**THE MECHANISM FOR WHY SARAH CAN SEE:**
When Sarah's father was murdered by the Corrupt Policeman, his body was buried in the excavation area — the site that was about to be cemented over as part of the regeneration. The killers believed his body would be disappeared forever — buried under concrete, gone.

But his body was placed in ground that contains the Old Gang's unconsecrated grave. Sarah's father physically TOUCHES the Old Gang buried beneath. His modern body makes contact with Victorian bones.

This creates an unholy, unique convergence of spiritual energy. The dead of the unconsecrated grave — the Old Gang, the historical figures, the criminals and the destitute — flow through Sarah's father. And through her father, to Sarah.

**This is why Sarah can see what others can't.** She is not a "chosen one." She is not psychic. She is not special. She is connected to the Old Gang through BLOOD AND BURIAL — her father's body touching their grave. The spiritual energy flows: Old Gang → father → Sarah. She feels the Old Gang because her father is lying among them. She senses the cold because her father is cold. She notices what others miss because the dead are communicating through the one body in their grave that is connected to a living person.

**What this means:**
- Sarah's sensitivity to the Old Gang isn't arbitrary — it's physical, specific, earned through tragedy
- Her colleagues can't see it because they don't have the connection — their fathers aren't in the ground
- As the excavation disturbs the grave more, the connection strengthens — Sarah sees more, feels more, drifts further into the old world
- When the drills reach her father's body, the connection reaches its peak — and her father returns
- The connection also explains why the Old Gang respect Sarah — they sense one of their own (her father) in her

**The killers' irony:** The Corrupt Policeman buried the body there to make it disappear. Instead, he created the connection that will eventually expose him. The cover-up is the catalyst. The murder is the mechanism. The thing he did to hide the truth is the thing that brings the truth back to life.

## The City of London — Ancient Places

The True Old Gang don't meet in modern London. They meet in places that have ALWAYS been there.

### The London Stone
111 Cannon Street. Most Londoners don't know it exists. Oldest artefact in London. A meeting place — the Old Gang know the Stone.

### The Oldest Pubs
Ye Olde Cheshire Cheese (Fleet Street), The George Inn (Southwark — the only surviving galleried coaching inn, National Trust), Ye Olde Mitre (Holborn, est. 1546). These aren't heritage attractions to the Old Gang — these are their locals.

### The Members' Clubs
White's (est. 1693), Brooks's, the Athenaeum, Livery Halls. The True Old Gang's natural habitat. The Ginger Urchin walks into one to meet a City figure — opulence surrounding a scrawny street urchin. That image IS the class argument.

### The Departure Points
Thames docks — Wapping, Rotherhithe, Pool of London — where emigrant ships left for America. Places of permanent goodbye. "This is where my father lived. I never saw him ever again."

### Why NOT Modern Locations
The Old Gang meet ONLY in places that have continuously existed since their era. Not Shoreditch House, not the Shard. The members' clubs, ancient pubs, the Stone — these survived everything. The Old Gang walk in as if they never left.

## The Old Gang's London — The Ancient Map

The Old Gang know THEIR London — rookeries, cesspits, cholera, child labour, mass prostitution, and spectacular theatre. They use OLD names and follow routes that no longer exist.

### Ancient Place Names
- **Jacob's Island** (Bermondsey) — notorious rookery, Dickens set Bill Sikes' death here
- **St Giles Rookery** (West End) — 54,000 people in a few streets. 40+ per house. Life expectancy: 18.5 years.
- **Devil's Acre** (Westminster, near Parliament) — slum in the shadow of democracy

These names have been erased by regeneration. The Old Gang insist on using them — resistance against forgetting.

### The River Thames
The Old Gang drank from it, washed in it, scavenged from it. Pre-Great Stink (1858), it was an open sewer. They drink from the clean modern Thames out of instinct — horrifying modern onlookers.

### The Extreme Poverty They Come From
- 40+ people per terraced house, cesspits as foundations
- Children as young as 5 in factories, mines, chimneys — 12-18 hour days
- Families "chucking out" children to save the others
- One third of young London women (15-25) were prostitutes in 1851
- Life expectancy in worst slums: 18.5 years

### Prostitution and the Church
South Bank brothels ("stews") licensed by the Bishop of Winchester. Prostitutes known as "Winchester Geese." The Church profited from the sex trade while preaching morality. Institutional hypocrisy has precedent going back centuries.

### Theatre — The Old Gang's Netflix
Shakespeare performed at the Globe in SOUTHWARK — Old Gang territory. Theatre was mass entertainment. The Old Gang cannot see screens but CAN experience live theatre.

**The Globe Theatre Scene:** The Old Gang break into the reconstructed Globe on the South Bank. Complete rapture. The entertainment of their era, in a building recreated from their world. They were too poor to see a play when alive. Now they weep with joy. One of the most powerful scenes in the novel.

### The Old Gang and Race
Victorian rookeries were mixed — poverty was the leveller. Old Gang don't see racial colour. Their prejudices run on class/territory. Mixed-race gang without racial hierarchy confuses all modern frameworks.

### The Gang as Historical Inheritance
Elephant has ALWAYS had gangs. The lineage runs continuously. The Old Gang resume something that never stopped.

### Dead and Alive Friendships
The boundary is permeable. The dead and living can be friends, coexist, have ordinary relationships. Diamond Anne and the Goth Girl. Sarah and the Camberwell Man. Elephant has always been a place where the living walk on top of the dead. Now they walk alongside.

### The Old Gang's Internal World
- **Territory assertion:** They re-establish authority on Booth streets — enforcing on businesses, demonstrating violence. This is what street gangs do.
- **Internal rivalries:** Not all members liked each other alive. Resurrection doesn't erase grudges.
- **Generational conflict:** Parental figures (the Fagin) return expecting deference. The Old Gang refuse.
- **Police misinterpretation:** When gangs report "Old Gang's back, they're dead" — police prepare for the wrong gang war entirely.
