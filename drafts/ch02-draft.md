The call came at seven twelve. Sarah was already dressed, already caffeinated, standing at the window of her flat in Kennington with her coat over her arm, watching the light change over the rooftops the way it does in London in early spring — reluctantly, as if the city had to be persuaded into morning. Her phone buzzed on the counter and she crossed the kitchen in three steps, read the text, picked up her keys.

The site was ten minutes from the station, off New Kent Road. She could have waited, driven from the nick with whoever they assigned, done it properly. Instead she walked. She wanted to see Elephant at this hour — before the commuters filled it, before the coffee shops opened their hatches, before the area put on its daytime face. She wanted to see it the way it actually was at seven in the morning: half-built, half-demolished, the cranes still and the hoardings blank and the old estates standing in the grey light like patients in a waiting room, not knowing if they'd been called yet.

New Kent Road was wide and charmless, the kind of London street that existed primarily as a route to somewhere else. Traffic was already building. She turned off at a gap in the hoardings where a uniform stood with his hands in his stab vest, looking cold and bored in the way only a PC on scene guard can look — a specific combination of authority and discomfort that they must teach at Hendon.

"DC Healy. I'm on the case."

He checked her warrant card with the thoroughness of someone who had nothing else to do. Lifted the tape. She ducked under.

The construction site was bigger than she'd expected. Three or four acres of churned earth, bordered by hoardings on three sides and the skeletal frame of a part-built tower on the fourth. The ground had been excavated in stages — she could see the layers, like a diagram in a geology textbook. Topsoil, then rubble, then clay, then something darker. Deeper. The machines were silent. A digger sat with its bucket raised, frozen mid-bite. Portakabins lined the north edge, their windows lit against the grey.

The body was at the eastern end of the site, in a trench that might have been three metres deep. She stood at the lip of it and looked down.

Young male. Black. Late teens, maybe early twenties. He was on his back, one arm thrown out, one leg bent at an angle that said he'd fallen or been dropped rather than laid down. He wore a puffer jacket — North Face, dark blue — and trainers that looked new. There was blood. A lot of blood. It had pooled beneath him and darkened the earth, and in the early light it looked almost black, indistinguishable from the soil.

Sarah had seen bodies before. In training, in her previous posting, on the two occasions she'd been called to a scene as the most junior officer available while everyone senior was busy with something that mattered more. She knew what a stabbing looked like. She knew the geography of knife wounds — where people aimed, where they hit, what the blood did afterwards.

This was different.

The wounds were wrong. Not wrong in a way she could immediately articulate — wrong in a way she felt before she understood. A modern stabbing was usually one or two wounds, sometimes more in a frenzy, but concentrated. Chest, stomach, back. The attacker going for the kill or panicking and stabbing repeatedly in the same area. There was a logic to it, even in chaos. Territory, revenge, fear — modern knife crime had patterns, and those patterns were legible if you knew how to read them.

This boy had been cut open. Not stabbed — opened. The wounds ran across his torso in lines that were almost deliberate, almost precise, as if the person who'd done this had been taught a different kind of violence entirely. There were cuts on his hands — defence wounds, she thought at first, but then she looked more carefully and saw that the cuts on his palms were too clean, too symmetrical. Not the ragged tears of someone grabbing at a blade. Something else. Something she didn't have a framework for.

She crouched at the edge of the trench. Behind her, she heard voices — more officers arriving, the forensic team setting up, the crime scene manager whose name she'd already forgotten giving instructions about cordons and pathways. She didn't turn around. She looked at the body.

The boy's face was unmarked. Whatever had been done to him, they'd left his face alone. She didn't know why that bothered her more than anything else, but it did. It suggested something — not rage, not panic, but intention. A code she didn't know.

"Nasty one."

She looked up. A man in a forensic suit was descending into the trench via an aluminium ladder, carrying a case. He nodded at her without interest — another detective, another body, another morning in Southwark.

"Gang?" he asked, not really asking.

"I don't know yet."

"It's always gang down here." He snapped on his gloves. "Who's SIO?"

"I think I am."

He looked at her properly for the first time. Took in the newness. The warrant card still on its lanyard rather than clipped to a pocket. The coat that was too clean for a building site.

"First one?"

"No."

He nodded, neither believing nor disbelieving, and got to work.

---

Sarah climbed out of the trench and walked the perimeter. The site manager was standing by the portakabins, a heavy man in a yellow hard hat and a high-vis jacket that strained across his shoulders. He was drinking tea from a flask and had the look of someone calculating how much this was going to cost him per hour.

"How long before we can get back on?" he said, before she'd introduced herself.

"I can't tell you that yet. I need to understand the timeline. When was this area last worked?"

"Yesterday evening. Lads knocked off at six. Night security does a walk-round at ten and again at two, but they don't come down into the trenches — they check the perimeter, the cabins, the machinery. Anyone could've come in over the hoarding after dark. Happens all the time. Rough sleepers, scrap metal thieves, kids."

"And this morning?"

"The crew started at six thirty. Davo — Dave Merchant, one of my groundworkers — he went down into the trench to check the levels before the concrete pour. Found him." He paused. "Davo's in cabin three if you need him. He's not in a great way."

Sarah wrote it down. "The excavation here — how deep does it go?"

"Depends on the phase. This section, we're down about three metres for the foundations. Over there" — he pointed to the eastern edge, where the trench deepened — "we went deeper last week. Hit some complications."

"What kind of complications?"

"Bones."

Sarah looked at him.

"Happens more than you'd think," he said. "This is Southwark. You can't dig a hole without finding someone's granny. We called the council archaeologist. She came out, had a look, said they're old — could be Victorian, could be older. Not recent, anyway. We're waiting on a report before we can pour that section."

"Can I see?"

He led her across the site, picking a path between ruts and puddles and stacks of rebar. The eastern edge of the excavation dropped away more steeply, and at the bottom — visible even from above — she could see them. Bones. Not a skeleton laid out neatly but fragments, a scatter of pale shapes against the dark earth, emerging from the clay like the ground was giving them up reluctantly. A femur. The curve of what might have been a pelvis. Something smaller she couldn't identify.

"The archaeologist reckons it's an old burial," the site manager said. "Unconsecrated. No coffins, no markers. Just bodies in the ground."

Sarah stared at the bones. They were less than ten metres from where the boy's body lay. Different depths — the bones deeper, older, part of the earth itself. The boy on the surface, barely cold. Two layers of death in the same hole. The archaeologist's dead and this morning's dead, separated by a hundred years and three metres of London clay, brought together by a man with a digger who was trying to pour a foundation.

"The boy's body — was it near the bones? Same trench?"

"Same trench, different level. The bones are at the bottom. The lad was on top, like he'd been dropped in. Or fallen."

Or placed, Sarah thought. Put there. In the same ground.

She didn't say it. She thanked the site manager and walked back toward the cordon. The forensic team was working in the trench now, the white suits bright against the dark earth. More officers had arrived — she could see Ravi at the outer cordon, talking to a uniform, and further back, behind the tape, the small crowd that always gathered at crime scenes, drawn by the blue lights and the silence and the particular electricity of something having gone wrong.

---

She almost missed the woman.

The crowd was sparse — a dozen people, maybe fifteen, standing behind the police tape on New Kent Road. Most were on their phones. A man in a delivery driver's uniform was trying to see past the hoarding. Two women with pushchairs stood together, murmuring. A teenage boy filmed on his phone with the practiced disinterest of someone who filmed everything and cared about none of it.

The woman was at the edge of the crowd, not in it. Small. Old — properly old, the kind of old that London was losing, the kind that remembered things nobody wrote down. She wore a coat that might once have been good and a scarf tied under her chin. She wasn't looking at the crime scene. She was looking at Sarah.

Sarah met her eyes. The woman didn't look away. There was no confusion in her face, no curiosity, no shock. She looked like someone who had been waiting.

Sarah walked over. "Do you live locally?"

"All my life, love."

South London. Not the gentrified version — the old version, decades deep, the accent that was disappearing from Elephant along with the estates and the old pubs and the people who remembered what had been here before the hoardings went up.

"Did you see anything this morning? Or last night?"

"I didn't need to see anything." The woman looked past Sarah toward the construction site. Toward the trench. "That's not a new gang. I know new gangs. That's not what this is."

"What do you mean?"

"That's the Old Gang."

She said it simply. Three words. No drama, no emphasis. The way you'd say the bus is coming or it's going to rain. A fact about the world so obvious it barely needed stating.

"The Old Gang?"

"You won't have heard of them. They were here before your lot. Before all of this." She gestured at the hoardings, the cranes, the glass towers rising behind them. "They were here when this was all black. When the maps said don't go."

Sarah opened her mouth to ask another question but someone called her name — Ravi, waving from the cordon. She turned, held up a hand to say one minute, turned back.

The woman was still there. Still watching. Still calm.

"What's your name?"

"You don't need my name, darling. You need to listen. You dig up the ground, you dig up what's in it. That's how it's always been."

Sarah took out her notebook. "If you could just—"

"Excuse me, Sarge? The DCI's on the phone." A uniform appeared at her elbow. Sarah looked at him, looked back at the woman.

"I'd like to talk to you again. Can I get a—"

"You know where to find me." The old woman pulled her coat tighter and walked away, slowly, down New Kent Road, in the direction of the old estates. The ones still standing. The ones that hadn't been dug up yet.

---

Back at the site, Ravi fell in beside her.

"Who was that?"

"Witness. Local. Said something about an old gang."

"Old gang like... OGs? Older lot running things from inside?"

"No. Old as in... old. Historical."

Ravi looked at her. Waited for the punchline. When it didn't come, he grinned. "Right. So we're looking for Jack the Ripper. Brilliant. I'll put out a description — tall, Victorian, probably wearing a top hat."

"She wasn't joking."

"Neither am I. The DCI wants an update. What do we know?"

Sarah told him what she knew: the timeline, the site manager's account, the body's position in the trench, the bones beneath. Ravi listened, nodding, professional.

"So gang killing, dumped at the construction site overnight. Fits the pattern — these sites are dead zones after dark, no CCTV once you're past the hoarding. Someone brought him in, did the business, left him."

"The wounds are wrong," Sarah said.

"Wrong how?"

She tried to describe it. The precision. The lines across the torso. The unmarked face. The defence wounds that didn't look like defence wounds. Ravi listened, and she watched his face do the thing that faces did when she talked like this — the slight tightening, the polite attention that was actually patience, the look of a person waiting for her to finish so they could offer the simpler explanation.

"Could be a message," he said. "Signature killing. Some of these lot have specific ways of doing things. It's branding, basically. You do it a certain way so everyone knows who did it."

"This isn't branding. This is something different."

"Different how?"

She didn't know. She couldn't explain it. The violence was from somewhere she didn't recognise — not the postcode wars, not the county lines escalation, not the domestic rage or the pub fight gone wrong. It was intimate and extreme and somehow formal, as if the person who'd done this had been operating within rules she'd never learned. A code from somewhere else.

"I don't know yet," she said. "But I want the post-mortem fast."

"I'll push it. Listen, about the old woman—"

"I know."

"It's just — every estate's got one. The old dear who remembers when it was all fields. They talk about the old days, the old gangs, the old whatever. It doesn't mean anything. It's just what old people do."

"I know."

But she didn't know. That was the problem. The old woman hadn't talked like someone remembering. She'd talked like someone recognising.

---

Sarah stayed at the scene until midday. She watched the forensic team work, spoke to Davo the groundworker — a big man who kept saying "I just went down to check the levels" as if repeating it would make the body disappear — and made notes about the site's access points, the hoarding gaps, the routes in and out. She spoke to the council archaeologist on the phone, a brisk woman called Dr Kemp who confirmed the bones were historical, probably mid-to-late Victorian, and that unconsecrated burials in this part of Southwark were "not uncommon — criminals, suicides, paupers, prostitutes, anyone the church wouldn't have."

Criminals in the ground. A dead boy on top of them. The same trench.

She stood at the edge of the excavation one last time before she left. The sun had come through briefly, catching the glass of the new developments to the south, and for a moment the site looked almost clean — just earth, just work, just the ordinary process of digging a hole and filling it with something new. The cranes resumed their slow rotation. A truck reversed through the gate, beeping.

But the cold.

That was the thing she couldn't file. The air temperature was twelve degrees — she'd checked, because she checked everything. Twelve degrees, light breeze from the south-west, dry. Not warm but not cold. Jacket weather. Normal.

The trench was cold. Not cool the way underground spaces are cool. Cold. A specific, settled, patient cold that had nothing to do with depth or shade or the absence of sunlight. She'd felt it when she crouched at the lip looking down at the boy. She'd felt it when she stood near the bones. She'd felt it — and this was the part she couldn't explain, the part she wouldn't put in her report — she'd felt it move. Not a draught. Not wind through a gap in the hoarding. Something else. A cold that seemed to come from the ground itself, rising through the clay, through the bones, through the dark earth, reaching for the surface the way water rises through a crack.

Twelve degrees. She checked again. She was right. The weather was the weather.

The cold was something else.

She turned and walked to the gate, past the portakabins, past the uniform on scene guard who was eating a sandwich now, past the hoarding with its artist's impression of the finished development — people on balconies, trees, a café, sunlight that didn't exist yet — and out onto New Kent Road.

Behind her, the trench waited. The bones below. The blood above. The cold between them, patient and still, as if it had been there for a very long time and was only now beginning to remember what it was for.
